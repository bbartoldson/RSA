# Step-by-Step Analysis

## Understanding the Experimental Setup

**Drug Mechanisms:**
- **CPT (Camptothecin):** Inhibits DNA Topoisomerase I → Topoisomerase I normally relieves positive supercoiling ahead of RNAP II → CPT causes torsional stress buildup → **slows RNA Polymerase II elongation**
- **TSA (Trichostatin A):** Inhibits histone deacetylases (HDACs) → prevents histone deacetylation → causes **histone hyperacetylation** → more open chromatin

## Interpreting the Results

**Experiment 1 (CPT alone):**
- Expected product: 2300 bp (full-length transcript)
- Observed product: 2000 bp (300 bp shorter)
- Conclusion: Slowing RNAP II promotes **alternative splicing** (likely exon skipping), producing a shorter isoform

**Experiment 2 (CPT + TSA):**
- Observed: Strong 2300 bp band + faint 2000 bp band
- Conclusion: TSA **rescues normal splicing** despite CPT still being present (RNAP II is still slowed)

## Key Mechanistic Inference

The critical observation is that TSA reverses CPT-induced alternative splicing even though CPT's effect on RNAP II speed persists. This rules out RNAP II elongation rate alone as the primary driver.

**Proposed pathway:**
1. CPT → slow RNAP II → increased torsional stress → promotes histone **deacetylation** at the alternatively spliced locus
2. Deacetylated histones → **chromatin condensation** → nucleosome repositioning over a specific exon
3. Nucleosome-occluded exon → hidden from spliceosome → **exon skipping** → 2 kb product

**Why TSA rescues:**
- HDAC inhibition → histones remain **hyperacetylated**
- Open chromatin → the exon is accessible to splicing machinery
- Normal splicing restored (2.3 kb) — even with slow RNAP II

Since TSA (specifically targeting histone deacetylation) fully overrides CPT's splicing effect, the **chromatin condensation driven by histone deacetylation** — not merely RNAP II speed — is identified as the main driver of gene X alternative splicing.
