Problem:

HEK293T cells were treated with 2.5 µM CPT, a DNA topoisomerase I inhibitor, after which the RNA for gene X was quantified with RT-PCR and agarose gel electrophoresis. This time, the resulting electrophoresis result shows a strong band at 2.3 kb, along with a faint band at 2000 bp. Based on these experiments, what process appears to be the main driver of gene X alternative splicing?

The 300 bp difference between the predicted 2.3 kb and observed 2.0 kb indicates a specific exon is being excluded in the CPT-only condition.

### Understanding What Each Drug Does

**CPT (Camptothecin):**
- Inhibits DNA Topoisomerase I by trapping the Topo I–DNA covalent cleavage complex
- This generates unresolved positive supercoiling ahead of RNA Polymerase II (RNAPII)
- RNAPII elongation is stalled or kinetically altered
- DNA damage signaling can also activate chromatin-modifying enzymes (including HDACs)

**TSA (Trichostatin A):**
- Inhibits class I/II Histone Deacetylases (HDACs)
- Promotes global **histone hyperacetylation**
- Results in more open, accessible chromatin

### Key Mechanistic Reasoning

The critical insight is that **TSA—an epigenetic/chromatin modifier—almost completely reverses CPT-induced exon skipping**, restoring the full-length 2.3 kb transcript. This logic chain follows:

1. **CPT treatment** → activates HDAC activity (or HDAC-dependent chromatin changes) at gene X's alternatively spliced exon → **histone deacetylation → compact chromatin**
2. **Compact chromatin** → alters RNAPII elongation kinetics at the alternative exon (via the kinetic coupling model) and/or reduces spliceosomal accessibility to the exon's weak splice sites
3. Result: **exon skipping** (2.0 kb product)

When **TSA is added:**
- HDACs are inhibited → histones remain **hyperacetylated** → **open, accessible chromatin**
- RNAPII elongation kinetics are restored through the alternative exon
- Spliceosome now has adequate time/access to recognize the alternative exon's splice sites
- Result: **exon inclusion** dominates (strong 2.3 kb), with only a faint residual skipping band (2.0 kb) due to CPT's torsional effects

### Applying the Kinetic Coupling Model

The **kinetic coupling (window of opportunity) model** explains co-transcriptional splicing:
- Chromatin compaction (deacetylated histones) → altered RNAPII elongation speed → insufficient opportunity for weak/alternative splice sites to be recognized → **exon skipping**
- Open chromatin (acetylated histones) → normal/restored RNAPII kinetics → adequate time for spliceosome assembly at the alternative exon → **exon inclusion**

### Conclusion

Since TSA (HDAC inhibitor → histone hyperacetylation → open chromatin) dominates the CPT-induced splicing effect, the data indicate that **HDAC-mediated histone deacetylation**—and the resulting chromatin compaction—is the primary driver of gene X alternative splicing. This operates mechanistically through the kinetic coupling of RNAPII elongation rate to co-transcriptional splice site selection.

FINAL ANSWER: Histone deacetylation (HDAC-mediated chromatin compaction), which alters RNA Polymerase II elongation kinetics at the alternative exon (kinetic coupling model), driving exon skipping—as evidenced by TSA (an HDAC inhibitor promoting histone hyperacetylation and open chromatin) reversing CPT-induced alternative splicing and restoring full-length transcript production.
